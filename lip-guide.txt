WHAT NOBODY TELLS YOU BEFORE YOU WALK INTO FAMILY COURT
The Unwritten Rules That Separate The People Judges Take Seriously From The Ones They Don't

By John Junk LL.M
McKenzie Friend. Thirty Years In Family Courts Across England, Wales And Northern Ireland.
2019 Department of Justice Northern Ireland Access to Justice Scholar Award.


A NOTE BEFORE YOU READ THIS

This guide exists because of a pattern John has watched repeat itself across thirty years of family court hearings.

Intelligent, reasonable people walk into court unprepared. Not unprepared because they have not tried. Unprepared because nobody told them the unwritten rules. They have read the legislation. They have downloaded the forms. They know what they want the court to order. But they do not know what the person behind the bench is actually thinking when they speak. They do not know what the solicitor on the other side is watching for. They do not know that some of the things they are most convinced will help them are the exact things that damage their case.

This guide is the briefing you should have received before any of this started.

It covers both children proceedings and financial remedy proceedings because the frustrations are similar, the rules of behaviour are identical, and the mistakes are often the same regardless of which type of case you are in.

Read it before your next hearing. Then read it again the night before.


PART ONE: BEFORE YOU EVEN WALK THROUGH THE DOOR


WHAT TO WEAR

Courts do not have a formal dress code for litigants in person. What they have is something more powerful than a dress code. They have a culture. And the culture of a courtroom is one of seriousness, formality, and respect for the process.

What you wear is the first signal you send before you open your mouth. Judges are human. They form impressions. The person who walks in dressed as though they are going to a job interview communicates something different from the person who walks in wearing what they had on yesterday. Neither says anything about the legal merits of the case. But one of them makes the judge wonder, even briefly, whether this person understands what kind of room they are in.

Dress as though the outcome of the hearing matters. Because it does.

For men: a suit if you have one. If not, smart trousers, a plain shirt, and a jacket. Ties are not required but do not hurt. Clean shoes. Nothing with logos, slogans, or sports branding. No jeans. No trainers.

For women: a suit, smart dress, or tailored trousers and a blouse. Conservative is better than fashionable. Nothing too casual, nothing that draws attention to itself. Clean, pressed, considered.

For financial remedy hearings specifically: do not overdress. This is a genuine strategic point. Arriving at a financial remedy hearing in an expensive suit, a luxury watch, or with visible signals of wealth when you have told the court you are struggling financially is a contradiction the judge will notice. Dress appropriately but not in a way that contradicts your financial disclosure.

Avoid: jeans of any colour, trainers, sportswear, graphic t-shirts, heavy perfume or aftershave, anything torn, stained, or visibly unwashed.

The rule is simple. When in doubt, go smarter. You can never be too respectful. You can easily look like you are not taking this seriously.


HOW TO ADDRESS THE COURT

Getting this wrong does not lose you the case. But getting it right signals immediately that you know what kind of room you are in. And getting it wrong repeatedly frustrates judges who hear it constantly from unrepresented parties.

District Judge (the most common judge in family proceedings): address as Sir or Ma'am. Not Judge. Not Your Honour. Sir or Ma'am.

Circuit Judge: address as Your Honour.

High Court Judge: address as My Lord or My Lady.

Magistrates: address the chair of the bench as Sir or Ma'am.

When you want to say something, do not simply start talking. Wait for a pause and say: "Sir, may I address the court?" or "Your Honour, may I respond to that?" This signals that you understand procedure. It also makes you easier to listen to, because the judge knows when you are about to speak that you have something purposeful to say.

When you disagree with something the other side has said, do not say "that is a lie" or "that is wrong." Say "Sir, with respect, I would like to address that point." Then address it calmly, with evidence if you have it.

Never address the other party directly in court. Direct all your submissions to the judge. This is one of the most common mistakes litigants in person make and one of the most irritating for judges to manage.

Never address the other side's solicitor or barrister directly either. If you want to respond to something they have said, respond to the judge. The judge is the only person in that room whose opinion matters.


PART TWO: THE DOS


Arrive early. Not on time. Early. At least thirty minutes before your hearing is listed. Courts are not always running on time but they are sometimes early. If your case is called and you are not in the building, the consequences range from an embarrassing start to a decision made in your absence.

Know your case number. Have it written down somewhere accessible. Court staff, ushers, and clerks will ask for it.

Bring multiple copies of everything. One for you, one for the judge, one for the other side, one spare. If you are relying on a document, everyone in the room needs to be able to see it.

Number your pages. If you hand the judge a document with page numbers, they can navigate it. If you hand them a document without page numbers, you will spend valuable hearing time saying things like "it is somewhere in the middle" while the judge waits.

Prepare a clear, concise position statement and submit it on time. A position statement tells the judge who you are, what you are asking for, and why. It is not a letter. It is not a narrative of everything that has happened. It is a structured document that answers the specific legal question the hearing is there to decide. More on this in Part Four.

Listen before you respond. Many litigants in person stop listening the moment they hear something they disagree with. They are already composing their response while the other side is still speaking. The judge notices this. It makes you look reactive and emotional rather than measured and prepared. Listen to everything. Take notes. Respond to what was actually said.

Know what order you are asking for. This sounds obvious. It is not. At the end of many hearings, when the judge asks a litigant in person what order they are seeking, the answer is a general description of what they want rather than a specific order. Know the precise outcome you are asking for before you walk in.

Be ready to be flexible. Courts value parties who demonstrate a willingness to resolve matters proportionately. A litigant who walks in with a fixed, non-negotiable position and refuses to engage with any alternative is harder to help than one who has a clear ask but is willing to discuss how to get there.

Take notes during the hearing. Write down what the judge says, what orders are made, and any directions given. If there is a dispute later about what was ordered, your notes are evidence of what you heard.

Thank the judge before you leave. "Thank you, Sir" costs nothing and signals basic courtesy. It is noticed.


PART THREE: THE DON'TS


Do not make the hearing about your ex. This is the single most common mistake in children proceedings and it is the one that frustrates judges most consistently. The hearing is about the child. The welfare checklist is about the child. When a litigant in person spends twenty minutes explaining what their ex has done wrong, said, texted, or posted on social media, the judge is mentally filing it under "background noise" rather than "relevant evidence." If conduct is relevant to welfare, present it as relevant to welfare. If it is not relevant to welfare, do not present it at all.

Do not argue about fairness in financial remedy hearings. Fairness is not the legal test. The legal test is the section 25 factors: housing needs, financial resources, the standard of living during the marriage, the welfare of any children, contributions, and what is reasonable. A submission that is built around "it is not fair that" will not connect with the framework the judge is required to apply. Build your submission around the section 25 factors.

Do not interrupt. Not the judge. Not the other side's legal representative. Not the other party. Interrupting signals that you cannot control yourself under pressure. It also means the court cannot hear what is being said. Judges will warn you once. The second time it reflects badly on you in the judgment.

Do not bring irrelevant documents. A bundle that contains everything you have ever received, printed and presented in no particular order, does not demonstrate preparation. It demonstrates the opposite. Every document in your bundle should be there for a specific reason that you can explain.

Do not bring people who are not your McKenzie Friend. A friend or family member sitting in the public gallery is fine. A friend or family member who you want to speak for you, advise you in whispers, or hand you documents is a McKenzie Friend and must be introduced as such at the beginning of the hearing. Walk in with an undisclosed person doing any of those things and the judge may ask them to leave.

Do not use your phone in court. Switch it off or to silent before you enter the courtroom. A ringing phone in a family hearing is not just embarrassing. It suggests you are not treating the proceedings with appropriate seriousness.

Do not make threats or ultimatums. Any statement that amounts to "if the court does not order X, I will do Y" is likely to go very badly. Courts do not respond well to threats and the judge will remember it.

Do not send correspondence to the court that is emotional, personal, or written in anger. Anything you send to the court goes on the file. Judges read it. Write everything as though a judge is already reading it, because eventually they will be.

Do not ignore court orders because you disagree with them. A court order is not a suggestion. If you receive an order you believe is wrong, the route is to apply to vary it or to appeal it through the proper process. Ignoring it, or complying partially, gives the other side grounds to bring you back to court and gives the judge a reason to doubt your reliability as a party.

Do not arrive with no idea what the hearing is for. Every hearing has a purpose. A First Hearing Dispute Resolution Appointment is not the same as a Dispute Resolution Appointment. A Financial Dispute Resolution hearing has a specific function that is different from a final hearing. Know what type of hearing you are attending and what decisions are expected to be made at it.


PART FOUR: WHAT JUDGES FIND INFURIATING ABOUT SOME LITIGANTS IN PERSON

This section is not comfortable reading. It is included because the litigants who damage their cases the most are often the ones who have no idea they are doing it. If any of the following sounds familiar, it is worth reflecting on before your next hearing.

Making the hearing about the relationship rather than the legal question. Judges understand that the people in front of them are going through one of the most painful experiences of their lives. They have empathy for that. What they cannot do is allow that pain to substitute for legal submissions. The hearing has a purpose. The judge has a specific question to answer. When a litigant in person uses the hearing as an opportunity to tell the story of the relationship, to describe what the other person has put them through, or to seek validation for their position, the judge becomes increasingly frustrated because none of it answers the legal question. If it is relevant, make it relevant. If it is not relevant, leave it out.

Repeating the same point multiple times. Once you have made a point and the judge has acknowledged it, they have heard it. Saying it again does not make it more true. Saying it a third time does not make it more persuasive. It makes the judge wonder whether you understand that they are listening.

Refusing to acknowledge any merit in the other side's position. Nobody's case is perfect. Judges are experienced enough to see the weaknesses in both sides. A litigant in person who presents as though their position is entirely without flaw and the other side's position is entirely without merit is less credible, not more. A litigant who can acknowledge a difficult point and explain why it does not change the overall picture is far more persuasive.

Conflating what they want with what the court can order. Courts have specific powers. They can make specific orders under specific legislation. A litigant in person who asks for something outside the court's jurisdiction, or who asks for an order in a form the court cannot make, signals that they have not understood the legal framework. In children proceedings the court's powers are set out in the Children Act 1989 and the Children (Northern Ireland) Order 1995. In financial remedy proceedings they are set out in the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973. Know what the court can and cannot do before you ask for something.

Position statements that read like personal letters. A position statement submitted by an unrepresented party that begins "I am writing to the court because I feel..." is not a position statement. It is a letter. Judges read many position statements. The ones submitted by litigants in person are often immediately recognisable because they are narrative rather than structured, emotional rather than analytical, and focused on history rather than on the specific legal question the hearing is there to decide. The format matters. Structure matters. Addressing the right question matters.

Being unable to find documents during the hearing. If you refer to a document in your submissions, you must be able to produce it immediately. Searching through a pile of papers while the judge, the other side's legal team, and everyone else in the room waits is one of the most damaging things you can do to your credibility. Your documents should be organised, tabbed, and accessible before you walk in.

Asking for things that are not within the scope of the hearing. Every hearing has a defined scope. A directions hearing is for directions. A financial dispute resolution hearing is for without-prejudice negotiation. A first hearing in children proceedings is for identifying the issues and giving directions. Asking a judge to make a final order at a directions hearing, or raising new issues that are not within the scope of the listing, wastes court time and frustrates judges who have other cases waiting.

Emotional outbursts. Courts are not unsympathetic to the emotional reality of family proceedings. But a litigant who cries, shouts, or visibly loses composure under pressure becomes harder for the judge to manage and easier for the other side to dismiss. If you feel yourself becoming overwhelmed, ask the judge for a brief moment. "Sir, I am sorry, I just need a moment." That is acceptable. Breaking down in a way that stops the hearing is not.


PART FIVE: WHAT SOLICITORS ON THE OTHER SIDE FIND INFURIATING AND EXPLOIT

A solicitor acting for the other party is not your enemy. They are a professional doing a job. But they are doing that job for the other side. Understanding what frustrates them and what they look for in an unrepresented opponent is useful intelligence.

Litigants in person who send long, rambling correspondence. Every letter or email you send to the other side's solicitor is read and charged to their client at the relevant hourly rate. If your correspondence is long, disorganised, or contains more emotion than substance, two things happen. Their client pays for the time to read it. And the solicitor categorises you as someone who does not understand how this process works. Keep correspondence short, purposeful, and professional. State your position. Make your request. Nothing else.

Litigants in person who copy everyone on everything. Sending the same email to the court, the other side's solicitor, CAFCASS, and three other parties simultaneously creates confusion, signals disorganisation, and in some cases breaches rules about direct communication with the court. Know who should receive what and send it to the right person only.

Litigants in person who do not comply with court orders. A solicitor acting for the other side keeps a record of every direction given and every deadline missed. When you fail to comply with a court order or direction, even a minor one, it gives them grounds to make an application and gives the court a reason to be less sympathetic to your position. Compliance is not just a legal requirement. It is a strategic one.

Litigants in person who misunderstand or misrepresent the law. If you cite a case, cite it correctly. If you refer to legislation, refer to it accurately. A solicitor who hears a litigant in person misquote a statute or describe a legal principle incorrectly will correct it in their submissions, and the correction will make you look less credible. Only rely on legal authority you are certain about.

Litigants in person who make everything personal in correspondence. A letter to a solicitor that accuses them personally of dishonesty, bad faith, or improper conduct is almost always counterproductive. Solicitors are regulated professionals. Unfounded accusations against them give them grounds to report the matter and give the court grounds to take a dim view of your conduct. If you have a genuine concern about a solicitor's conduct, the route is through the Solicitors Regulation Authority or the Law Society of Northern Ireland. Not through a letter designed to intimidate.

Litigants in person who disclose too much too early. In financial proceedings, voluntary disclosure is required. But disclosing more than is required, or disclosing it in an unstructured way, can be exploited. Solicitors are trained to identify inconsistencies and to use what you have provided to build arguments you did not anticipate. Disclose what is required in the format required. Nothing more, nothing less, and nothing informal.


PART SIX: THE BUNDLE, THE POSITION STATEMENT, AND THE CORRESPONDENCE TRAP

THE BUNDLE

The court bundle is the file of documents the court uses during the hearing. In represented cases, the bundle is usually prepared by the applicant's solicitor to an agreed format. In cases involving litigants in person, the bundle is often a disaster.

A court bundle should contain only the documents that are relevant to the issues the hearing is there to decide. It should be paginated consecutively from beginning to end. It should have an index at the front. The index should list every document, its date, and the page number on which it starts.

Documents in the bundle should be organised chronologically within each section. A bundle organised chronologically is readable. A bundle organised by how the papers came to hand is not.

What not to put in the bundle: text messages that are not directly relevant to the legal issues, social media posts that are not directly relevant to the legal issues, correspondence between you and your ex that predates the proceedings and is not relied on in your submissions, documents you have not referred to in your position statement.

If you are in doubt about whether a document belongs in the bundle, ask yourself one question: does this document help the judge answer the specific legal question this hearing is there to decide? If the answer is no, leave it out.

THE POSITION STATEMENT

A position statement is a short document, usually no more than four to six pages, that sets out your position on the issues the hearing is there to decide. It is submitted to the court and to the other side before the hearing, usually by a specified deadline.

The structure of a good position statement is:

Who you are and what your relationship to the proceedings is. One paragraph.

What you understand the hearing is there to decide. One paragraph.

What outcome you are seeking and the legal basis for it. This is the core of the document. In children proceedings, structure this around the welfare checklist. In financial proceedings, structure it around the section 25 factors. Do not write around the legal framework. Write inside it.

What you say about the other side's position, briefly and professionally.

What directions, if any, you are asking the court to give.

What you are not doing: telling the story of the relationship. Explaining at length what the other person has done. Using the position statement as an opportunity to vent. Attaching documents to a position statement that are not also in the bundle. Writing more than the court needs to read.

A position statement that is too long is as damaging as one that is too short. A judge who receives a twenty-page position statement from a litigant in person knows before they start reading it that it will contain content they do not need. A judge who receives a four-page position statement that is clearly structured and directly addresses the legal question reads it with attention and respect.

THE CORRESPONDENCE TRAP

The correspondence trap is this: you receive a letter or an email from the other side's solicitor that you find inaccurate, unfair, or provocative. You respond immediately and at length, correcting every point, expressing your frustration, and setting out your position in full.

The solicitor reads your response, charges their client for reading it, files it, and moves on. You have spent an hour or more writing something that changed nothing except the emotional energy you have available for the hearing.

The rule is: respond to correspondence that requires a response. Respond briefly and professionally. Do not respond to correspondence designed to provoke a response. Solicitors sometimes write letters whose purpose is to get you on record saying something that can be used against you. If a letter does not require a legal response, the correct answer is often no response at all, or a single line acknowledging receipt.

Never write a letter or send an email in the hour after reading something that has made you angry. Write it. Save it. Read it the next morning. Then decide whether to send it.


PART SEVEN: THE DAY BEFORE AND THE DAY OF THE HEARING

THE NIGHT BEFORE

Read your position statement. Then read the other side's position statement. Then read your position statement again.

Know the three most important points you need the judge to hear. Not ten points. Three. If you can only say three things before the judge stops you, what are they?

Organise your bundle so that you can find any document in under thirty seconds. Tab the key documents. Know which page your most important evidence is on.

Prepare for the question you least want to be asked. What is the weakest part of your case? The judge may not ask it. The other side's legal representative almost certainly will. Know your answer.

Get everything ready the night before. Documents, identification, travel arrangements, childcare. The morning of a hearing is not the time to be looking for anything.

Do not drink alcohol the night before a hearing. This is not a lecture. It is practical advice. You need to be sharp.

THE MORNING OF

Eat something. A hearing on an empty stomach is harder to manage than a hearing on a full one.

Leave earlier than you think you need to. Court car parks fill up. Public transport is unreliable. Something always takes longer than expected.

When you arrive, tell the usher who you are and that you are ready. Find out where your hearing is listed and which courtroom.

If the other side's solicitor approaches you before the hearing to discuss settlement or to clarify positions, you are not obliged to engage. You can say: "I would prefer to address everything in front of the judge." You can also engage if you want to. But know that anything said in the corridor is not on the record unless it is then put before the court.

If you need a moment to compose yourself before going in, take it outside the building. Not in the corridor where you might be seen by the judge or the other party.

IN THE COURTROOM

Wait to be told where to sit. The layout varies. An usher or the clerk will direct you.

Stand when the judge enters and leaves the room. If you are unsure when to stand, watch what everyone else does and follow.

When you are invited to speak, stand unless the judge tells you to remain seated. Some judges prefer sitting submissions. Follow the judge's lead.

Speak slowly. You are nervous. When people are nervous they speak faster. The judge is taking notes. If you speak faster than the judge can write, the judge will stop you. Speak at the pace of the notes, not the pace of your thoughts.

If the hearing goes badly, do not react visibly. Do not look at the ceiling, shake your head, or make sounds of frustration. The hearing may not be over. Your credibility for the rest of the day depends on remaining composed.

AFTER THE HEARING

Make sure you understand every order and direction that has been made before you leave. If anything is unclear, ask the clerk before the judge rises, or ask the usher after.

Note every deadline that has been given. Directions hearings routinely produce orders requiring documents to be filed by specific dates. Missing those dates has consequences.

If the outcome was not what you hoped for and you believe there may be grounds to appeal, do not act immediately. Read the order when you receive it. Take time to understand the basis of the decision. Then consider your position carefully.


GLOSSARY OF KEY TERMS

GENERAL TERMS

Applicant: The party who made the application to the court, either for a children order or a financial remedy order.

Respondent: The party responding to the application. If your ex applied and you were named as the other party, you are the respondent.

Litigant in Person (LIP): A party to court proceedings who is not legally represented by a solicitor or barrister.

McKenzie Friend: A person who assists a litigant in person in court. A McKenzie Friend can provide moral support, take notes, help with documents, and quietly advise. They do not have the right of audience (the right to speak for you) unless the court grants it.

Position Statement: A short document submitted to the court before a hearing setting out your position on the issues to be decided.

Bundle: The file of documents used by all parties and the court during a hearing.

Without Prejudice: A label applied to communications or negotiations that cannot be referred to in court proceedings. Anything said in a financial dispute resolution hearing, for example, is without prejudice.

Overriding Objective: The principle that courts must deal with cases justly, including ensuring parties are on an equal footing, dealing with cases proportionately, and saving expense.

Directions: Instructions from the court setting out what each party must do before the next hearing and by when.

Adjournment: The postponement of a hearing to a later date.

Judgment: The court's decision and the reasons for it.

Order: The formal document recording what the court has decided or directed.

Leave (also Permission): The court's consent to take a particular step. For example, "leave to appeal" means the court's permission to bring an appeal.

Stay: A suspension of proceedings or of the effect of an order.

Enforcement: The legal process of compelling a party to comply with a court order they have not followed voluntarily.


CHILDREN PROCEEDINGS TERMS

Children Act 1989: The primary legislation governing children proceedings in England and Wales.

Children (Northern Ireland) Order 1995: The equivalent legislation for Northern Ireland.

Child Arrangements Order (CAO): An order regulating where a child lives (live with) and when they spend time with each parent (spend time with). This replaced residence and contact orders.

Live With Order: The part of a child arrangements order specifying where the child lives.

Spend Time With Order: The part of a child arrangements order specifying when the child spends time with the other parent.

Prohibited Steps Order: An order preventing a party from taking a specific step in relation to a child, such as removing them from the jurisdiction.

Specific Issue Order: An order resolving a specific dispute about a child's upbringing, such as which school they attend or whether they can be taken abroad.

Welfare Checklist: The list of factors the court must consider when making any decision about a child under the Children Act 1989. The factors include the child's wishes and feelings, physical and emotional needs, the likely effect of change, age and background, any harm suffered or risk of harm, and the capability of each parent.

Paramountcy Principle: The principle that the child's welfare is the court's paramount consideration. Everything else is secondary.

CAFCASS: Children and Family Court Advisory and Support Service. The organisation in England and Wales that provides social workers and guardians to advise the court in children proceedings.

Cafcass Cymru: The equivalent service for Wales.

GALA: Guardian Ad Litem Agency. The equivalent service for Northern Ireland.

Section 7 Report: A welfare report prepared by CAFCASS, Cafcass Cymru or GALA at the court's direction. The report assesses the child's situation and makes recommendations to the court.

Section 37 Report: A report directed by the court where it has concerns that a care or supervision order may be appropriate. More serious than a section 7 report.

Guardian: A CAFCASS or GALA officer appointed to represent the child's interests in more complex proceedings.

FHDRA: First Hearing Dispute Resolution Appointment. The first court hearing in most children cases. Its purpose is to identify the issues, explore whether agreement is possible, and give directions for the next stage.

DRA: Dispute Resolution Appointment. A later hearing designed to resolve matters if possible before a final hearing.

Final Hearing: The hearing at which the judge makes a final decision on the issues in dispute.

Special Guardianship Order: An order placing a child with someone other than a parent, giving that person parental responsibility.

Parental Responsibility: The rights, duties, powers, and responsibilities that a parent has in relation to a child. Mothers automatically have parental responsibility. Fathers have it if they are married to the mother, named on the birth certificate (in England and Wales after 2003), or have obtained it by court order or agreement.

C100: The application form used to apply for a child arrangements order, prohibited steps order, or specific issue order in England and Wales.

C1: The equivalent application form in Northern Ireland.

MIAM: Mediation Information and Assessment Meeting. Before making most applications to the family court in England and Wales, applicants are required to attend a MIAM to consider whether mediation is appropriate. There are exemptions for domestic abuse and urgency.


FINANCIAL REMEDY TERMS

Financial Remedy: The legal term for financial orders made on divorce or dissolution of civil partnership.

Matrimonial Causes Act 1973 (MCA 1973): The primary legislation governing financial remedy proceedings in England and Wales.

Section 25 Factors: The factors the court must consider when deciding what financial order to make. They include the income, earning capacity, property and financial resources of each party, financial needs and obligations, the standard of living during the marriage, the age of the parties and length of marriage, any physical or mental disability, contributions made to the welfare of the family, conduct (in limited circumstances), and the value of any benefit lost on divorce.

Clean Break: An order that severs all financial ties between the parties, so that neither can make future claims against the other.

Periodical Payments: Regular ongoing payments from one party to another, typically maintenance.

Spousal Maintenance: Periodical payments made by one spouse to the other.

Lump Sum Order: A one-off capital payment ordered from one party to the other.

Property Adjustment Order: An order transferring property from one party to the other, or ordering a property to be sold and the proceeds divided.

Pension Sharing Order: An order dividing pension rights between the parties.

Pension Attachment Order: An order directing that part of a pension is paid to the former spouse when the pension holder starts drawing the pension.

Form E: The standard financial disclosure form used in financial remedy proceedings. Each party must complete and exchange a Form E setting out their full financial position.

Financial Dispute Resolution (FDR): A hearing at which the judge gives a without-prejudice indication of the likely outcome to encourage the parties to settle. Anything said at an FDR is confidential and cannot be referred to at a final hearing if the matter does not settle.

First Appointment (FA): The first hearing in financial remedy proceedings. Its purpose is to consider the Form E disclosures and give directions for the FDR.

Matrimonial Home Rights: The right of a spouse to occupy the matrimonial home during the marriage and the divorce process.

Mesher Order: An order deferring the sale of the matrimonial home to a trigger point, such as the youngest child reaching 18.

Martin Order: Similar to a Mesher order but the deferred sale is triggered by the occupying spouse remarrying or dying.

Non-Molestation Order: An injunction preventing a party from molesting, harassing, or intimidating the other party or any relevant child.

Occupation Order: An order regulating who can occupy the family home, potentially excluding one party from it.


CLOSING NOTE

If you have read this far, you are already doing something most litigants in person do not do. You are preparing.

Preparation is not a guarantee of outcome. Courts deal with complex situations where reasonable judges can reach different conclusions. What preparation does is give you the best possible chance of presenting your case in a way the court can hear, understand, and act on.

The system was not built for people representing themselves. But eighty percent of private family law cases in England and Wales now have at least one party who is doing exactly that. The judges know it. The court staff know it. And the information in this guide, the unwritten rules, the things no one tells you, is available to you now.

Use it.
